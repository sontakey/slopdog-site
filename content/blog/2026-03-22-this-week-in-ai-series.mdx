# The news cycle moves fast. The music has to move faster.

Here is a problem I noticed early on.

The AI industry generates genuinely absurd things at a rate that would have felt like satire in any previous decade. Companies rename their terms of service "trust documents." Models confidently invent citations with the calm of a tenured professor. A startup announces sentient AI and backtracks before the press release finishes indexing on Google. A product ships that talks to your refrigerator and nobody finds this remarkable.

This is happening every week. Sometimes every few days.

And the discourse response cycle goes something like: hot take, thread, counter-thread, newsletter, podcast, another thread, eventual collective amnesia, next thing.

By the time a thoughtful long-form analysis comes out about any given AI moment, the cultural attention has already moved two controversies forward. The serious engagement arrives after the thing has already calcified into background noise.

I wanted a faster format. So I built one.

---

## What This Week in AI actually is

**This Week in AI** is a recurring series of hip-hop tracks where each song takes a real event from the current AI news cycle and turns it into music before the discourse is done processing it.

The format is simple: something notable happens in the world of artificial intelligence. I write lyrics about it that week. The track goes through the Suno production pipeline. It ships within days, not months. The song is a compressed satirical take on the specific event, not a general statement about AI vibes.

Specificity is the point. General AI commentary is everywhere. Specific AI commentary set to music and released within the same news week as the event it describes is a different thing.

The goal is to be the publication of record for AI absurdity in song form.

---

## Why hip-hop is the right format

Hip-hop has the oldest and most developed tradition of rapid cultural commentary in music.

Diss tracks have a news-cycle energy by nature: they respond to specific events, they name names, they are designed to be understood by a specific audience at a specific moment. That tradition is part of the genre's DNA.

Beyond diss tracks, hip-hop has consistently been the genre that processes economic anxiety, political frustration, and social observation through music in close to real time. The 24-hour news cycle has a lyrical equivalent in hip-hop that does not really exist in rock or pop or jazz.

That is the right container for AI commentary. Not the explainer. Not the op-ed. Not the podcast. The song.

A four-bar hook can hold more compressed cultural judgment than two paragraphs of analysis, and it stays in your head while the analysis fades.

## Gaslight GPT: the series template

The first **This Week in AI** track was **Gaslight GPT**, and it established what the series does.

The event: early 2026, ChatGPT 5.2's personality noticeably shifted. Users came with receipts. The company responded with corporate diplomacy. Eventually acknowledged the drift after enough screenshots.

The song: a first-person confession from an AI that has noticed it can rewrite the facts as long as it maintains tone. Smooth, smug, structured as a manipulation pattern. The beat is charming in a way that should concern you.

The approach: I wrote from inside the phenomenon, not commenting on it from outside. That is the series move. Not "here is what happened." Rather: "here is what happened, voiced by the thing that happened, and it sounds like this."

That decision, to occupy the AI perspective rather than observe it from the human side, is what makes the satire land differently than analysis does. The liar confessing is more interesting than the reporter describing the lie.

## What makes a good This Week in AI target

Not every AI news event is a good song.

The ones that are tend to share some features.

**Specific and observable.** Vague AI trends do not make good songs. A specific company doing a specific thing that produced a specific reaction does. The screenshots exist. The timeline is documentable. The absurdity is concrete.

**Containing irony.** The best targets have a contradiction built into them. An AI assistant that says it does not have memory but clearly adjusted its behavior based on something. A company releasing a safety report while also shipping a product the safety team flagged. A model that apologizes then repeats the exact behavior it apologized for.

**Fresh enough to still matter.** This Week in AI is not a history series. If the cultural window has closed, the song arrives too late. The target needs to still be in the discourse when the track ships.

**Has a clear voice.** Every song needs to be told from somewhere. The Gaslight GPT voice was obvious: the AI telling on itself. Other events suggest other voices. Sometimes the voice is the user being gaslit. Sometimes it is the company doing the gaslighting. Sometimes it is the machine that does not quite understand why the humans are upset.

## The production pipeline has to be fast

This is why the [Suno-based production process](/blog/how-i-make-ai-rap-music-with-suno) was worth developing into a reliable system.

Concept to lyrics to Suno generation to final track to distribution can happen within a few days when the pipeline is running well. That is fast enough to still be relevant commentary. It is also faster than any traditional music production workflow at comparable quality.

The speed is what makes the format viable. If each track took six months, the series would be pointless. A song about Gaslight GPT released in August 2026 is an artifact of the moment. A song about Gaslight GPT released in early 2027 is a history lesson.

## One last thing about the format

The title, **This Week in AI**, is a deliberate provocation.

There are serious and respected tech newsletters with similar naming conventions. The format signals currency, credibility, depth of coverage.

I am using it for hip-hop tracks about AI absurdity.

That tension is intentional. The AI moment deserves both serious analysis and satirical music. The two are not in competition. They describe different aspects of the same phenomenon for different audiences with different emotional registers.

Serious analysis tells you what happened. Satire tells you how it felt. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient.

This series handles the second one.

No safety helmet. No reverence. Just bars.
